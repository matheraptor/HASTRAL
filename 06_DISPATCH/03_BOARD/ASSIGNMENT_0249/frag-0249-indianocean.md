# The Indian Ocean: Meteorological Shifts and Geopolitical Rivalries

Crossing the Indian Ocean toward the Malacca Strait introduces both seasonal weather risks and the complexities of regional power competition. May 2026 marks the onset of the Southwest Monsoon, a moisture-laden wind system that brings heavy rainfall and squally conditions across southern India and the Bay of Bengal.[30]

The development of a "Super El Niño" is the dominant climate driver for 2026, typically associated with reduced monsoonal rainfall but increased potential for severe, sudden storms in the North Indian Ocean.[30, 34] Mariners are advised that while total seasonal rainfall might be below average (92% of long-period average), the early season is prone to intense bursts of wind activity that can reverse trade winds and create hazardous sea states.[30, 32]

## India's Strategic Role and the SAGAR Doctrine

India has decisively asserted its role as a "net security provider" in the Indian Ocean Region (IOR). Under the SAGAR (Security and Growth for All in the Region) vision, the Indian Navy has deployed carrier battle groups—led by INS Vikrant and INS Vikramaditya—to provide close-protection escorts for critical shipping.[35, 36] In May 2026, India informed Tehran that Indian-flagged vessels would transit the region under active naval guidance without paying "protection fees" to the IRGC.[35] This assertive posture has established a "Safe Corridor" for vessels affiliated with India or carrying essential food and energy supplies.[35, 37]
