# The Straits of Malacca and Singapore: Congestion and Crime

The passage through the Straits of Malacca and Singapore (SOMS) remains the most critical chokepoint on the route to Japan. Over 94,000 ships crossed the straits in 2024, and traffic in 2026 has reached record levels as ships reroute from other global hotspots.[29] This density, combined with the need for ships to reduce speed in the narrow waterway, makes the SOMS particularly vulnerable to maritime crime.[29]

## Piracy and Armed Robbery Trends Q1 2026

The IMB reported a sharp fall in global piracy in Q1 2026, but the Singapore Straits continue to account for the highest concentration of incidents.[28] Most incidents are "Category 4" (least significant), involving opportunistic theft without injury to the crew.[44, 45] However, the use of weapons, including knives and guns, was reported in six cases during the first quarter.[28]

## Port of Singapore Reliability

Singapore remains a premier transshipment hub, but in May 2026, it is experiencing "schedule reliability challenges".[46] Vessel delays elsewhere in the network are causing "vessel bunching" and omitted port calls as carriers attempt to recover their schedules.[46, 47] For humanitarian cargo, this increases the risk of "cargo rolling"—where containers are left behind for subsequent sailings—potentially delaying the arrival of aid in Japan.
