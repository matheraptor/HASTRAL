# Potential Stop-over III: Sri Lanka as a Transshipment and Relief Hub

The Port of Colombo is a vital intermediate stop-over, strategically located along the East-West trade lanes. In 2025, Colombo set a new record by handling 8.29 million TEUs, reinforcing its position as South Asia's leading transshipment hub.[38] For humanitarian logistics, however, the port presents a paradox of efficiency and administrative backlog.

## Relief Cargo Accumulation and Administrative Friction

As of mid-May 2026, the Sri Lanka Port Authority (SLPA) reports that approximately 294 containers of relief cargo are stored at the port.[39] These include consignments for UNICEF, IFRC, and World Vision, alongside many private donations.[39] A major bottleneck identified in early 2026 was the implementation of additional military inspections of cargo, which slowed clearance rates from 600 containers per day to just 30, resulting in the perishing of food items.[40] While the SLPA grants demurrage waivers for government-distributed aid, UN agencies often face accumulating charges at both the port and Bandaranaike International Airport (BIA).[41]

### Diversification of Shipping Routes

Japan is currently using Sri Lanka as a key node for diversifying its shipping routes. Vessels like the Idemitsu Maru have been confirmed to stop in Sri Lanka before heading to Japanese ports like Nagoya, bypassing the Strait of Hormuz by using routes through the Red Sea and Suez Canal.[43] For a humanitarian vessel, Colombo offers:

* **Consolidation**: The ability to pick up stranded relief cargo for Southeast Asia or Japan.[39]
* **Infrastructure**: Deep-water capability to handle ULCVs and transshipment to regional feeders.[38]
* **Logistics Coordination**: Access to the Logistics Cluster’s regional coordination mechanisms.[39]
