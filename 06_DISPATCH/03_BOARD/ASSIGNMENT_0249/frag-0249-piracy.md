# The Gulf of Aden and the Resurgence of Somali Piracy

The transit from the Red Sea into the Indian Ocean requires passage through the Gulf of Aden, currently one of the most volatile maritime zones in the world. Maritime security data from May 2026 indicates a troubling return of Somali piracy, categorized as "Severe".[5, 6] This resurgence is largely attributed to the redirection of international naval assets to address the Hormuz and Houthi crises, leaving a security vacuum in the Somali Basin.[27]

As of May 14, 2026, three merchant vessels are currently held by Somali Pirate Action Groups (PAGs):

* **Oil/Products Tanker**: Held since April 21, 2026.[5]
* **General Cargo/Cement Carrier**: Held since April 26, 2026.[5]
* **Oil Tanker**: Hijacked on May 2, 2026, approximately 10 NM off the Yemen coast and diverted toward Somali territorial waters.[5]

The IMB Piracy Reporting Centre (PRC) notes that perpetrators are successful in boarding vessels in 94% of attempts.[28] Pirate groups have become increasingly sophisticated, using AIS data to identify slow-moving, high-value, or vulnerable targets.[29] For a humanitarian vessel, strict adherence to Best Management Practices (BMP) and the maintenance of a high-alert watch are mandatory. While pirate-held vessels are generally contained within Somali waters and do not pose a direct "mother ship" threat to all shipping, the likelihood of PAG activity remains high at extended ranges.[6]
