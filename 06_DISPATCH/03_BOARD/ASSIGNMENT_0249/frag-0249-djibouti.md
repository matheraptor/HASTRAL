# Potential Stop-over II: Djibouti and the Horn of Africa Corridor

South of the Sudanese coast, Djibouti represents the most strategically significant logistics node on the route. Djibouti’s deep-water ports, specifically the Doraleh Multipurpose Port (DMP) and the SGTD container terminal, serve as the primary gateway for landlocked Ethiopia, handling over 90% of its trade.[18, 19] In May 2026, Djibouti is managing a surge in diverted vessels while maintaining a commitment to "lifeline" corridors.[20]

## Port Capacity and Strategic Prioritization

The DMP reported in April 2026 that it has sufficient capacity to handle incoming cargo rerouted from the Persian Gulf due to the Hormuz closure.[18] The facility operates five simultaneous berths and maintains massive storage infrastructure, including 85,000-ton grain and 185,000-ton fertilizer silos.[18] The DPFZA has issued a mandate to prioritize all cargo destined for Ethiopia, particularly fertilizer, ahead of the upcoming Kiremt (rainy) season.[19, 20]

### Humanitarian Needs and WFP Pipeline Alerts

Despite its logistical prowess, Djibouti is facing its own humanitarian constraints. The WFP reported a 61% pipeline break in April 2026, putting resilience and refugee programs at imminent risk.[24, 25] Tensions in the Middle East have delayed deliveries of essential vegetable oil and wheat flour, leading to price spikes.[24] A stop-over in Djibouti could provide:

* **Agricultural Inputs**: Bulk fertilizer for the Ethiopian highlands.[19]
* **Food Staples**: Vegetable oil and Super Cereal Plus for pre-positioned stocks.[25]
* **Energy and Repair**: Jet fuel and diesel for regional energy distribution hubs and maritime repair services.[22]

The IMF and World Bank have emphasized that while Djibouti’s ports are resilient, the "quiet architecture" of the corridor—the coordination of rail, dry ports, and customs—is what prevents external shocks from becoming national emergencies.[23, 26] A humanitarian vessel docking at DMP would benefit from the "Fast-Track" protocols for Ethiopian-bound cargo, ensuring rapid discharge and inland transit via the electrified railway.[20]
