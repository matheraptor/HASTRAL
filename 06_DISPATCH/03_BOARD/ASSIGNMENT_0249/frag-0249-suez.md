# Mediterranean Departure and Suez Canal Navigational Protocols

The journey begins in the Eastern Mediterranean, a region currently assessed at a "Moderate" maritime risk level.[5, 6] While the waters surrounding Limassol remain relatively stable, the broader geopolitical tension following the escalation of the US-Israel-Iran conflict in February 2026 has introduced new layers of complexity to Mediterranean logistics.[1] Commercial and humanitarian vessels departing from Cyprus must operate within the heightened vigilance framework established by regional naval operations, including EUNAVFOR Atalanta and Aspides, which have shifted resources to secure the approach to the Suez Canal.[7]

The Suez Canal remains the primary arterial route for this transit, maintaining an "Operational" status despite the volatility in the southern corridors.[6] Navigation through the canal is governed by the Suez Canal Authority (SCA) Rules of Navigation, which were significantly updated to accommodate larger vessels and more complex cargo profiles.[8] Southbound transit is organized into two daily convoys, with ships required to radio the harbor when within 15 nautical miles of the Fairway Buoy at Port Said.[8] The transit typically spans 11 to 16 hours at a regulated speed of 8 knots to mitigate bank erosion.[8]

While the canal is operating normally, the de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz on February 28, 2026, has fundamentally altered Mediterranean shipping patterns.[1] Non-humanitarian commercial traffic has largely rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 to 14 days to standard voyages and causing a "vessel bunching" effect at Mediterranean transshipment hubs.[1, 9] For a humanitarian vessel, this ensures that while the canal is physically open, the administrative and scheduling environment is increasingly crowded as regional authorities prioritize essential commodities like food, fuel, and medical aid.[4]
